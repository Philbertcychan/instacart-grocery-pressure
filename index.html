Where Canadian grocery inflation is concentrated

Instacart's business sits on top of grocery spend — basket size, order frequency, and willingness to pay a delivery markup all respond to how much pressure shoppers feel at checkout. This tracks that pressure directly from Statistics Canada price data, then applies published demand-elasticity research to estimate what it could mean for volume.

Pick a category — scenarios use published Canadian food price elasticity estimates

What this is and isn't: all elasticity estimates are now Canadian. Meat uses recent national retail data (Diakité, 2024 — beef, poultry, and lamb own-price elasticities from 2019–2022). The other 7 categories use a Government of Canada study (Pomboza & Mbaga, 2007, Agriculture and Agri-Food Canada) based on 2001 household survey data — fully Canadian, but older. Where that study reports two related food groups (e.g. bakery and cereal), the range shown spans both; where it reports one, low/base/high collapse to a single value. Treat all of these as illustrative ranges for how demand could respond, not a validated forecast — none of these studies measure grocery delivery demand specifically.
Built with public data — Statistics Canada CPI (Table 18-10-0004-01) and Canadian food price elasticity research (PMC, AIDS model 2006/07 & 2009/10). Independent analysis, not affiliated with Instacart or Statistics Canada.
